# Campaign Bible — Sword & Sorcery (Working Title)

> **Living document.** Names, factions, and details will change as the vision sharpens. Sections marked **[TBD]** are placeholders awaiting decisions. Do not treat any name here as final.

---

## Tone & Register

**Competent mortality.** Not a power fantasy, not a meatgrinder. The characters are professionals — people who have trained, chosen this life, and equipped themselves for work that most people won't do. They walk into places that kill ordinary travellers and come back. Not because the world shows them mercy, but because they know things and can do things that ordinary people can't.

Death is real and meaningful. It happens when players overreach, make bad decisions, or run out of luck in ways better preparation might have prevented — not because the world rolls dice against them arbitrarily. When a character dies, it matters.

The world is dark and genuinely dangerous. It is also comprehensible. Threats have logic. Preparation has value. Experience compounds. The space between "this world is brutal" and "these characters are capable" is where the interesting decisions live.

**Tone anchors:** Conan the Barbarian, Game of Thrones, Battle Brothers, Kenshi. Gritty, grounded, morally ambiguous. No chosen ones. No destiny. No safety net. But also not nihilistic — loyalty, courage, and competence mean something.

**What the characters are not:** Heroes in the narrative sense. They are sellswords, tomb robbers, exiles, and survivors who are better at staying alive in dangerous places than most people. The world knows it. They get called when there's a problem in the March that needs someone willing to go in.

## The World — Overview

Life on this continent has never been gentle. The land is dangerous by nature: dense ancient forests, mountain passes that kill in winter, diseases that travel faster than armies, things in the wilderness that have been there longer than human civilization and are indifferent to its concerns.

What the great empires provided — roads, law, cleared trade routes, military enforcement against threats — was always fragile infrastructure laid over a world that didn't care about it. That infrastructure is now failing.

**Layered history.** The world has three historical strata:

1. **The Precursors** — a civilization that predated the current empires by centuries, possibly longer. Spanned most of the known world. Collapsed for reasons nobody living fully understands. Their ruins are everywhere. Their works are superior to anything current craftsmen can produce.

2. **The Three Empires** — the powers that rose on the Precursors' foundations, eventually went to total war with each other, and contracted into their current diminished states. All three still exist. None are what they were.

3. **The current fragmented world** — city-states, frontier settlements, ungoverned March territory, tribal peoples who were never absorbed into the empires, and the complicated human mess of politics, trade, faith, and survival that fills the gaps.

## The Precursors

An empire that once spanned the known world. Not a myth — their roads still run under current roads, their foundations underlie current cities, their ruins dot the March in configurations that don't match any current architectural tradition. Their written language has been partially deciphered. Their intentions, mostly, have not.

**What they were:** Organised, sophisticated, and deeply entwined with sorcery in ways the current world doesn't fully replicate. They didn't use magic the way current practitioners do — as a dangerous, costly exception. It was infrastructure. That may be why they're gone.

**What happened:** Unknown. The Ash Compact's official position: they pushed too far and were consumed by what they built. The cults' whispered belief: they completed something, and whatever they completed is still here. The Old Faith's account: they tried to kill the old powers and failed. Nobody has a complete picture. The ruins themselves suggest something sudden — not decline but interruption.

**What they left:**
- Roads and foundations that current civilisation built on without fully understanding
- Ruins throughout the March, ranging from overgrown wall fragments to vast complexes that have barely been entered
- Artifacts — weapons, tools, materials — that are far superior to current craft and worth fortunes
- Writings that take years to parse and are almost never entirely good news
- Something else. What wakes in the deep ruins is **[TBD]** — but expeditions that go too far in don't always come back with living members.

**Precursor ruins in play:** Two distinct ruin types exist in the March. Imperial-era ruins (50–100 years old, politically complicated — someone's grandfather built that, someone's faction has a claim) and Precursor ruins (centuries old, alien in character, built to purposes nobody currently understands). The distinction matters. Looting an imperial ruin is grave-robbing. Looting a Precursor ruin is archaeology with teeth.

## The Three Empires [TBD: Names]

All three empires still exist. None are what they were. They contracted after the Last War into their core holdings and have been managing internal crises ever since. Their former borderlands and frontier territories — much of what is now called the March — they no longer control.

**Empire A — The Old Blood [TBD: Name]**
Coastal, trade-built, elaborate bureaucracy and a long tradition of scholar-priests who codified law and kept records. Their capital is still functioning and still impressive — but their inland territories are gone. Roads they built now run through lands they don't control. Their caravans need armed escort on routes that were once safe. Current crisis: a succession dispute twelve years unresolved, two claimants with genuine legal arguments and private armies.
*Aesthetic anchor: Mediterranean / Near Eastern. Stone cities, olive groves, elaborate legal tradition, a faith focused on contract and obligation.* **[TBD: Refine]**

**Empire B — The Riders [TBD: Name]**
Expanded fast on horseback, extracted tribute and loyalty through fear and speed. Never deeply rooted. When the wars went badly, their outer territories simply stopped paying tribute and there was nothing to send to collect it. What remains: a rump state of fortress-cities on the steppe edge, a cavalry force still genuinely dangerous, and a ruling class that hasn't accepted what it is now. Relationship with former subject peoples: complicated, bitter, occasionally violent.
*Aesthetic anchor: Steppe / Central Asian. Cavalry culture, felt and leather, ancestor shrines, pragmatic about the Old Faith.* **[TBD: Refine]**

**Empire C — The Federation [TBD: Name]**
Never as centralised as the other two. Always an alliance of city-lords and highland chieftains who gave allegiance to a High King in exchange for trade rights and mutual defence. The High King still exists. The allegiance is now ceremonial. City-lords and chieftains act independently, make their own deals, and occasionally war with each other. Simultaneously the most stable (small powers adapting) and the most chaotic (no central authority to stop anything).
*Aesthetic anchor: Northern European / early medieval. Timber halls, iron and wool, competing warlords, a strong warrior-honour tradition.* **[TBD: Refine]**

## The Six Settlements [TBD: Names]

Six settlements the characters can call safe havens, in the sense that there is law, trade, and somewhere to sleep that probably won't kill you. More settlements may be discovered or established as play expands.

**1. The Home Base [TBD: Name]**
A crossroads town on the edge of the March. Old enough to have stone walls; young enough that the gate timbers are still new. Population: merchants between runs, mercenaries between contracts, refugees from failed homesteads, a scholar or two poking at nearby ruins, people who have nowhere better to be. The local lord keeps order with a light hand because she needs the trade. The March begins half a day east. Expeditions depart from here. This is where you drink before you go and drink more when you come back.

**2. Steppe Town [TBD: Name]**
Low buildings, wide streets built for horses, a permanent market camp at the walls that has been there so long it has its own customs. Warrior-culture, horse-herding, pragmatic about nearly everything including their bone-speakers — hedge-magic practitioners who read signs and communicate with remnants of the dead. Nobody calls them sorcerers. Everyone knows what they are. The Ash Compact has not successfully established a foothold here, and the locals' position on that is expressed through horses and spears.

**3. The Sorcerer-Lord's City [TBD: Name]**
The wealthiest of the six settlements, and the most unsettling. The ruling lord — possibly the same person who has ruled for longer than any current record shows, possibly a dynasty maintaining a careful fiction — keeps the city prosperous, protected, and fed in a region that should support none of those things. There is a structure beneath the old palace that no one enters. There are disappearances among the poor that are officially migration. The city is clean, well-ordered, and subtly wrong in ways that take a few days to identify. The Ash Compact has a chapter house here and says nothing about the palace. That silence is informative.
*The secret is [TBD] — something beneath the city that the lord contains and siphons. The city benefits; the cost is paid by someone.*

**4. The Port [TBD: Name]**
Rich, cosmopolitan, corrupt. Several factions control different districts; the city nominally has a council; everyone with money has private soldiers. The docks run at night. This is where things that shouldn't be bought or sold get bought and sold — old-world artefacts, sorcery services, information, people. The Ash Compact burns things here periodically. The city rebuilds and returns to business.

**5. The Mountain Fortress [TBD: Name]**
Controls the only reliable pass through the range. Isolationist, heavily stone-built, strongly Ash Compact-aligned — the local chapter has more real authority than the lord. Sitting on rich iron deposits. Will trade and will not ask about your religion, but they watch. Arriving with a known practitioner of sorcery makes entry difficult.

**6. The Delta Town [TBD: Name]**
Founded by refugees. Has not stopped being settled by refugees since. Built on platforms over brackish water, connected by rope bridges, expanded organically in every direction. The oldest families know things about the delta that newcomers die learning. Rumoured to house the largest surviving collection of pre-Ruin knowledge, in a building that is larger inside than the outside suggests. Nobody who lives there discusses this openly.

## The March

The March is the territory between and beyond the six settlements — former borderlands, tributary territories, frontier land that all three empires once claimed and none currently control.

It is not wasteland. It is ungoverned.

Evidence of former prosperity is everywhere: stone roads that need repair, irrigation channels going to seed, foundations of towns that burned and were not rebuilt. Some of it was always wilderness — deep forest, high passes, river delta — that the empires had only partially tamed. What makes it the March is the *absence*: no army clearing roads, no magistrate hearing disputes, no garrison discouraging the things in the old forests from drifting closer to where people sleep.

**Why it's opportunity.** Ruins of former prosperity contain things worth finding. Land grants from any of the three empires — all of whom technically still claim March territory — can be had cheaply because no one enforces them. Clear and hold a piece of it and the grant starts to mean something. Old Precursor ruins predate the empires entirely, and whatever is in them has been left alone long enough to be interesting.

**Why it's deadly.** Decades of ungoverned wilderness grows dangerous wildlife to fill the vacuum. Former imperial garrisons that went unpaid turned bandit. Cult activity flourishes without law to suppress it. The battles of the Last War were fought here — some of those battlefields are still strange.

**The road problem.** Trade between settlements requires moving through the March. Roads exist, mostly. They are only somewhat safe. Different routes carry different risks — faster roads through old forest, slower roads that avoid known danger zones, passes that are closed in winter and contested in summer. The economic pressure of trade is constant: merchants need to move goods, guards need paying, and every route is a calculation between speed and safety.

## Magic

Sorcery is real, costly, and dangerous. It does not come from study or divine favour in any clean sense — it comes from proximity to things that predate human civilisation, from bargains with powers that are not interested in the bargainer's wellbeing, and from the Precursor legacy: tools and techniques for doing things that the people who invented them understood and current practitioners do not.

**The tiers:**

*Hedge-magic* — bone-reading, herb-curses, warding signs, minor divination. Practitioners skim the surface of something old and mostly don't understand what they're skimming. The cost is slow and cumulative: shortened life, persistent unease, a sense of being watched that never goes away. Common enough that most settlements have someone who does it, by one name or another.

*True sorcery* — reshaping reality, commanding things, grand workings. Requires real sacrifice — not metaphorical. Something given up permanently: time, memory, flesh, relationship, years. The practitioner chooses what; the power chooses whether it's enough. Corruption is visible over time. High practitioners are recognisably wrong in ways that vary by individual, as if the body is being slowly rewritten.

*The deep workings* — what the Precursors did. Nobody does this intact. Nobody.

**The Precursor connection.** Current sorcery is essentially attempting to use tools made by people who understood them, without that understanding. The Precursors built something into the world — a capacity, a channel, a wound, depending on whose account you believe — and practitioners are drawing from it. What that means for the long term is one of the questions the setting does not answer cleanly.

**Magic on the battlefield.** The Last War saw sorcery used as a weapon at scale. The specific places where major workings were employed are still wrong: ground that won't grow crops, animals that avoid it, humans who feel it before they can see it. This is not the whole March — just those places. Concentrated, specific, and old enough that most people know to avoid them.

## The Ash Compact

Founded in the aftermath of the Last War by survivors who correctly identified sorcery as what broke everything. Their founding purpose was twofold: contain the practice, preserve the record of what it cost. Both were reasonable goals.

They are older now, larger, and more interested in power than their founders were. Their inquisitors are effective, feared, and increasingly willing to define "sorcery" expansively when it is politically convenient. They are not wrong that magic is dangerous. They are not right about everything else.

The Compact has chapters in most settlements. Their influence varies: welcome in the mountain fortress, entrenched in the port's politics, tolerated in the sorcerer-lord's city for reasons that imply compromise, unwelcome on the steppe, operating underground in the delta town.

**The internal fault line.** The Compact's founding scholars argued for preserving knowledge of what sorcery does so it could be recognised and countered. The inquisitors who do the actual work have trended toward destruction — burn the library so no one learns from it. This tension has not been resolved. There are still scholars in the Compact who know things the inquisitors would burn them for knowing.

**In play:** The Compact is not simply a villain faction. It is an institution with legitimate grievances, genuine hypocrisy, internal divisions, and real power. Dealing with them requires navigation, not just opposition.

## The Cults

Not unified, not organised, not always aware of each other. Several distinct strands:

**Completion cults.** They believe the Last War was a beginning, not an ending — that the Precursors were doing something and it wasn't finished, and that finishing it is the correct goal. Actively dangerous. Actively hunted by the Compact, which for once has reasonable justification.

**Preservation cults.** Old mystery traditions that predate the empires, attempting to keep Precursor knowledge alive through the Compact's purges. Not inherently malevolent. Increasingly driven to desperate measures to survive. Some have made compromises to do so.

**Power cults.** People who want what sorcery offers and are willing to pay. The most common, least organised, most easily recruited by the other two. Often the front door through which people enter darker affiliations.

**The Old Faith.** Not quite a cult — more the substrate everything else grew from. The steppe people's bone-speakers practice it openly. The delta town's oldest families observe pieces of it quietly. It predates the current mainstream religion, acknowledges the old powers without necessarily worshipping them, and treats the dead as genuinely present. The Compact considers it heresy. The Old Faith considers the Compact a recent and temporary problem.

## Creatures of the March

The March is dangerous not because it is organised but because it is *alive*. Decades of ungoverned wilderness has produced things that fill the vacancy — apex predators, territorial entities that predate human settlement, animals warped by proximity to old sorcery sites, things that don't fit clean categories.

**Design principle.** Every creature creates a *specific situation*, not just a damage number. The danger is comprehensible once you know what you're dealing with. First encounter you survive by improvisation. Second encounter you survive by knowing. That knowledge has real value and can be shared, sold, or used as leverage.

**The Grove-That-Walks** *(Ancient Forest)*
A network organism. The visible form — massive, bark-covered, slow — is a surface expression of an underground root system extending hundreds of feet. Severs into smaller forms when damaged enough. Cannot be killed by hacking; requires finding and destroying the buried core, or fire. Its mind is genuinely alien — it is not aggressive in any purposeful sense, simply territorial in the way an immune system is territorial.

**The Stitched** *(Forest edges, old battle sites)*
Not a species — a condition. Animals caught near major sorcery sites, fundamentally altered. Each one is unique: wrong proportions, wrong behaviour, wounds that close too fast. No two are the same. The danger is that you cannot learn "how to fight a Stitched" — every encounter requires reading what this one is before you can respond correctly.

**The Risen Mire** *(Swamp, marsh)*
Bodies that go into swamp water under certain conditions don't decay. They fill and eventually move. Not intelligent, not malevolent — a wrong process completing itself. The danger: fallen party members who aren't recovered or burned may return as more of them. Managing the aftermath of a fight matters as much as winning it.

**The Listening** *(Deep marsh, dense forest)*
Nobody has seen one fully and survived to describe it. What's documented: it mimics sounds — distress calls, animal noises, the voices of people who have previously been in that territory. Experienced travellers know that when you hear something familiar in the deep marsh, it is probably not that. Separates groups. Leads individuals away in the dark.

**The Pale** *(Mountain passes, high altitude)*
Massive, white-furred, built for cold and altitude. Semi-intelligent in the way centuries of hunting humans makes something: it targets capable fighters first, retreats when the odds shift, returns when you think it's gone. The steppe people have individual names for Pales with established territories. Killing a named one is a significant deed. Fighting the same Pale twice means it has already adapted to you.

**Scree Drake** *(Mountain passes, ruins, mine shafts)*
Wingless, serpentine, venomous, nearly invisible against rock. Not large — dangerous because venom is slow and ruins the march home. The calculation after a bite: how far is the nearest settlement, what do you have, is the time saving worth the risk? They den in ruins and underground spaces as readily as natural crevices.

**The Echo** *(Precursor ruins)*
Something that inhabits deep Precursor ruins rather than having a body. Experienced as: floor that was stable that isn't, walls that weren't there that are, sounds without sources. It treats intrusion the way an immune system treats infection. Cannot be fought directly. Must be located — what it is anchored to — and that destroyed, or the party must leave. Scholars argue whether it is a creature, a remnant working, or something that has no existing category.

**The Accumulator** *(Old mines, ruined forges)*
A creature that has incorporated metal and stone into its outer layer over generations — not by design, but by growth. Its shell is salvaged weapons, armour fragments, ore, grown together. Metal weapons are less effective against it and in close quarters may be grabbed. The oldest Accumulators have absorbed Precursor-era materials; their bodies are accidental archaeological records.

**The Lean** *(Open roads, settled fringe)*
Bipedal. Roughly human proportions. Not human. They scout alone — one is seen once, at distance, and then gone. They attack in groups at night, targeting the edges of a camp, and they are disturbingly good at reading human behaviour: they know where the sentry stands and when he turns. Old road wisdom: they are drawn to cooked meat. Cold camps are safer.

**The Watcher** *(Any road, unpredictable)*
Not directly dangerous. Follows trade routes. Experienced as something at the edge of vision, always the same distance back, always there when you look. Horses panic. Pack animals refuse to move forward. It eventually acts — randomly, without apparent trigger — and then it's gone. Nobody agrees on what it is or what it wants. The uncertainty is itself the threat.

**Wrack** *(Old battle sites, sorcery-scarred ground)*
What living things become when caught in the radius of major sorcery. Not a species — a condition that animals, humans, and possibly vegetation can catch. Each Wrack is unique because the original creature was unique. Some are recognisable as what they were. Some are not. The battlefields of the Last War have the most of them.

## The Crises — World-Level Events

At intervals determined by faction pressure, player action, and GM judgment, one of the following escalates from background tension to active world-reshaping event. Each has warning signs that players can notice and respond to — or ignore.

**The Beast Tide**
Something disrupts the ecology of a large portion of the March — old predator territory collapses, a Precursor site activates something, something migrates through. The result: creatures pushed out of their normal ranges, concentrated pressure on settlements and roads, encounters that are denser and more desperate than usual. Not a single enemy but a pressure system.

**The Precursor Stirring**
Something wakes in the deep ruins. Not necessarily undead in the traditional sense — organised, tireless, operating on an agenda that predates current civilisation. Settlements near major Precursor sites are at risk of being claimed rather than destroyed. The nature of what stirs is deliberately unclear until it is directly encountered.

**The Imperial War**
One or more of the three diminished empires decides the time is right to reassert territorial claims. Armies move into March territory. Every settlement is forced to pick a side or defend its neutrality. Contracts become political. The characters' previous faction relationships suddenly have weight they may not have anticipated.

**The Compact's Crusade**
The Ash Compact launches a serious campaign — not local inquisition activity but organised force, moving settlement to settlement. Having a known hedge-witch in town is now a liability. Factions that have tolerated or harboured practitioners are being called to account. The cults are suddenly highly motivated to find allies. The characters' relationship to magic — their own, their contacts', what they've encountered in the March — becomes politically significant.

*Only one crisis is active at a time. Resolving one does not prevent another from building.*

## The Reactive World — Player Impact

West marches without persistent story arcs requires that players feel the weight of what they do. The world must respond visibly to their actions — not just GM narration, but tangible, lasting change.

**Small actions, visible consequences:**
- A dangerous road section cleared → merchants begin using it → a small camp appears, then a waystation
- A named Pale killed in the northern pass → the steppe people know, the pass opens for trade, the characters' names are associated with it
- Bandits left alone → they grow bolder, more merchants are hit, the lord raises bounties and is less willing to pay for other work
- A ruin mapped and its dangers documented → that information has trade value; other expeditions use it; the characters built something real

**Faction standing is persistent and visible:**
- Helping the Ash Compact once creates a contact; helping them repeatedly creates expectation and obligation
- Exposing the sorcerer-lord's dirty secret has political consequences that ripple through every settlement with Compact presence
- Earning respect from the steppe town opens routes, information, and potentially allies that aren't available through other channels

**The map is the record:**
- Territory the players have explored becomes known; what they found there persists
- Safe routes they established are safer because they made them so
- Locations they cleared stay cleared until something moves back in
- The difference between a map at session 1 and a map at session 20 should be visibly the players' work

**Death has weight:**
- Named NPCs remember who died and how
- Factions note when capable people don't come back from a job they recommended
- A character's reputation, contacts, and earned standing do not simply disappear — they are inherited by whoever picks up the thread, or they decay visibly if nobody does

**The goal:** Players who are away for a session return to a world that has moved, and can see both the consequences of what they did before and the evidence of what others have done in the meantime. The world is always in motion. Their actions are part of that motion.

## Character Backgrounds

Backgrounds define who a character was before they ended up at the home base, and directly shape their starting capabilities and knowledge. The backgrounds that exist describe the world's social fabric.

**Martial:** Former soldier, deserter, mercenary, disgraced knight, bodyguard. The Last War produced a surplus of trained fighters with no army to return to and no home waiting. The frontier pays for what they know.

**Trade & Road:** Caravan guard, river boatman, teamster, merchant's factor. Expertise in movement, routes, and reading danger on the road. Invaluable for the economic logic of the March.

**Criminal:** Bandit turned legitimate (or not quite), smuggler, fence, escaped prisoner. People with reasons to be where law is thin. They read dangerous situations well because they have been one.

**Craft & Knowledge:** Blacksmith, apothecary, archivist, surgeon. Skills with direct utility on the road. The apothecary who knows poisons knows antidotes. The archivist chasing Precursor ruins can also read what they find.

**Faith & The Occult:** Ash Compact acolyte, hedge-witch's apprentice, bone-reader, cult escapee. Connected to the world's religious tensions — either enforcing the mainstream order or surviving its attention. Built-in friction with each other and with situations involving magic.

**Dispossessed:** Exiled courtier, bastard heir, escaped serf, refugee from collapsed imperial territory. Displaced by the Last War or its political aftermath. On the frontier because they have nowhere else, or because something is following them.

**Wilderness:** Hunter, trapper, pathfinder, shepherd from the March's edge. Already fluent in terrain and threat. Often underestimated by the others. Usually right.

*All of these have a plausible reason to be at the home base specifically. The frontier collects people the settled world has used up, pushed out, or has no room for.*

## West Marches Structure

**One home base** to start. Any major settlement becomes a safe haven as players reach it and establish relationships there. The home base is where expeditions depart from and return to — it has a tavern, a blacksmith, a market, a notice board with work, and a lord who keeps order because she needs the trade.

**Sessions are expeditions.** Players organise a party, pick a direction, go, and come back — or don't. Each session is self-contained. No cliffhangers that lock half the table out of next week's game.

**The map is the campaign.** Explored territory becomes known and changes. Unexplored territory stays dangerous and unknown. The map at the end of the campaign should be visibly the work of the players who made it.

**Drop-in, drop-out.** Characters exist at the home base between sessions. A player who can't make it is simply not on this expedition. New players arrive as new characters who have just reached town. The world accommodates this naturally — the frontier always has new arrivals.

**No rescue.** If a party goes dark, they're gone. The world does not send help. This is what makes the characters' competence meaningful: they're the ones who would have been sent.
